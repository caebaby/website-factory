# Page System
## Inner Page Architecture for Wealth Advisory & Professional Services
## LLM-Agnostic Reference — editorial-luxury template

This document defines every inner page type a premium advisory site needs, the section order for each, the navigation architecture, and the CTA strategy across the full site. Read this before building any page beyond the homepage.

**Research basis:** Best-in-class architecture analysis of Aspiriant, Abacus Wealth Partners, Facet, Lake Road Advisors, Carson Wealth, and the conversion architecture patterns behind Superhuman, Typeform, and ConvertKit.

---

## Core Principle

**Every inner page has one job: advance the visitor toward the primary conversion goal** (assessment, call booking, or intake form). Pages that describe without directing are brochures. Brochures don't convert.

**The conversion path flows in one direction:**
```
Homepage → Niche/Service page → Trust-builder (About or Case Study) → CTA destination
```

Inner pages must pass the baton forward. Never let visitors stall or dead-end.

---

## Page Set Options

### Minimum Viable (3 pages beyond homepage)
1. About
2. Services Overview
3. Contact / Schedule

Without these three, the site cannot convert.

### Standard (6 pages)
Add to MVP:
4. Individual Service Detail page(s)
5. Team / Advisors
6. Resources / Blog hub

Covers 80% of conversion scenarios.

### Premium (9+ pages)
Add to Standard:
7. Case Studies hub + individual case study pages
8. Individual team bio pages
9. Persona-based landing pages (e.g., "For Business Owners", "For Pre-Retirees")
10. Assessment / Quiz landing page
11. Geographic or niche sub-pages

## Page Architecture: Section Orders

### About Page

**Emotional job:** Belonging and trust. Visitor must feel they've found their people before they've read the credentials.

**Rule:** Never open with founding year, AUM, or credential list. Open with the client problem the firm was created to solve.

| Order | Section | Content |
|-------|---------|---------|
| 1 | Mission hero | One sentence anchored in client outcome, not firm history. "We exist because..." |
| 2 | Origin story | Why this firm exists — a problem the founder saw and refused to accept. The villain is the industry status quo. |
| 3 | Philosophy | 3–4 principles that differentiate HOW they work (not what they do). Make these uncomfortable-specific. |
| 4 | Team preview | 2–3 headshots, names, roles — pulls to full Team page |
| 5 | Trust signals | AUM, years in business, client count, press mentions, awards. Factual only — no fabrication. |
| 6 | Transition CTA | "Meet the full team" or "See how we work with clients" |

### Services Overview Page

**Emotional job:** Recognition and relevance. Visitor thinks "this is exactly what I need."

**Rule:** Name the client problem first. Service names come second.

| Order | Section | Content |
|-------|---------|---------|
| 1 | Category hero | "We do X for people facing Y." Client-situation-first, not service-taxonomy-first. |
| 2 | Service card grid | 3–6 cards. Each card: client problem first, then solution name. Each links to individual service page. |
| 3 | Who it's for | Named client archetypes (not demographics). "The Business Owner Two Years From Exit." |
| 4 | Process teaser | 3-step "how we work" visual — reduces fear of the unknown |
| 5 | One proof element | Single short testimonial or stat. One is enough here. |
| 6 | CTA | "Not sure where to start? Take our assessment." |

### Individual Service Detail Page

**Emotional job:** Certainty and specificity. Right-fit visitors feel seen; wrong-fit visitors self-exit.

**Rule:** First sentence names the client's pain. Not the service name.

| Order | Section | Content |
|-------|---------|---------|
| 1 | Problem-first hero | Lead with the specific pain, not the service name. "You've built a business worth selling. The question is whether your exit will be tax-efficient enough to actually fund the next chapter." |
| 2 | Solution framing | How you approach this problem differently — one clear POV |
| 3 | What's included | Specific deliverables/activities. Not vague benefits. "Monthly rebalancing report + quarterly advisor call + tax-loss harvest review." |
| 4 | Who this is for | Explicit qualifier. Makes right prospects feel seen; others self-select out. |
| 5 | Case study or proof | One real-world scenario, anonymized if needed |
| 6 | FAQ | 3–5 objections answered directly. These are the real objections from sales calls. |
| 7 | CTA | "Schedule a conversation about [service name]" — specific, not generic |

Do NOT list credentials on service pages. Credentials belong on About.

### Team / Advisors Page

**Emotional job:** Personal connection. Visitors pick advisors on personality, not credentials.

**Rule:** The personal paragraph is the conversion driver. No exceptions.

| Order | Section | Content |
|-------|---------|---------|
| 1 | Team philosophy header | One sentence about how the team is built and why that benefits clients |
| 2 | Advisor cards grid | Name, title, photo, 1-line specialty, link to bio |

**Individual advisor bio page structure:**
```
1. Headshot (professional, not headshot-photographer-generic)
2. Professional credentials (CFP, CFA, etc.)
3. Areas of focus (client types and situations)
4. Personal paragraph — what drew them to this work, what they care about
5. "Work with [Name]" CTA with calendar embed or contact form
```

The personal paragraph converts. "I became a financial planner after watching my parents retire with far less than they expected, despite working hard their whole lives" gives visitors a reason to choose this specific person. Credentials establish qualification; personality establishes trust.

### Contact / Schedule Page

**Emotional job:** Zero friction. Remove every obstacle between intent and action.

**Rule:** Calendar embed is the primary element, above the fold. Contact form is secondary. Long intake forms kill conversion.

| Order | Section | Content |
|-------|---------|---------|
| 1 | Framing statement | What happens when you reach out — removes fear of the unknown. "Here's what to expect..." |
| 2 | What to expect | 3-step process: "15-min intro call → discovery meeting → proposal." Makes the invisible visible. |
| 3 | Primary conversion | Calendar embed (Calendly, Cal.com, or similar) above the fold |
| 4 | Secondary | Contact form: name, email, ONE qualifying question only. No more fields. |
| 5 | Footer trust | Hours, phone, office address, compliance disclosures |

Never put a long form as the primary option. Ask for name, email, and one question — then have a human do discovery on the call.

### Resources / Blog Page

**Emotional job:** Authority without pressure. Prove expertise through generosity.

**Rule:** Every resources page must have a conversion mechanism. Pure content with no next step is a traffic leak.

| Order | Section | Content |
|-------|---------|---------|
| 1 | Category filter bar | Topics aligned to CLIENT PROBLEMS — not content types. "Retirement / Tax / Business Exit / Estate Planning" — not "Articles / Videos / Podcasts / Guides." |
| 2 | Featured article | One hero-sized recent piece |
| 3 | Article grid | Date, category tag, title, 1-sentence summary. Clean and scannable. |
| 4 | Lead magnet callout | Mid-page email capture with a specific offer. "The Pre-Retirement Tax Checklist" beats "Subscribe to our newsletter." |
| 5 | Footer CTA | Back to services or assessment |

Wrong taxonomy: Articles / Videos / Guides / Podcasts  
Right taxonomy: Retirement / Tax Planning / Business Exit / Estate Planning / Investing

### Case Studies Page

**Emotional job:** Proof that removes objection. Visitors see themselves in the story.

**Rule:** Use client archetypes, not real names (unless written consent obtained). Structure every case study as a narrative.

| Order | Section | Content |
|-------|---------|---------|
| 1 | Positioning header | "Client results, not just promises." |
| 2 | Filter by situation | Filter by client type/situation — NOT by service. "Business Owners / Pre-Retirees / Corporate Executives" |
| 3 | Case study cards | Client archetype → The challenge → The outcome (measurable) |

**Individual case study page structure:**
```
Situation → Challenge → Solution → Measurable Results
CTA after every case study: "Is your situation similar? Let's talk."
```

DO NOT use client names without written consent. Reference: "The Business Owner Approaching Exit," "The Pre-Retiree With Concentrated Stock," etc.

## Navigation Architecture

### Primary Navigation (5–7 items max)

```
[Logo]    Services    About    Team    Resources    [CTA Button]
```

**Preferred client-outcome naming:**
- "How We Help" > "Services"
- "Get to Know Us" > "About"
- "Your Roadmap" > "Our Process"
- "Results" > "Case Studies"

**Never in primary nav:** Careers, Disclosures, Privacy Policy, Press (unless it's a primary trust anchor), anything that describes the firm without orienting to the client.

**Client Login:** Top-right corner as a utility element, visually separate from conversion navigation. Never in the primary nav — it splits hierarchy between prospects (who need to be converted) and existing clients (who already know where to go).

### Footer Navigation

Full sitemap including:
- All legal/compliance links (ADV, Privacy Policy, Form CRS, disclosures)
- All secondary resource links
- Careers
- Social links
- Address and contact
- Compliance disclaimer (required, full-width, below fold)

### URL Structure

Use descriptive folder paths:
- `/services/retirement-planning` not `/page?id=123`
- `/about/our-story` not `/about-2`
- `/team/jane-smith` not `/team/advisor1`

Clean URLs signal premium. Ugly URLs signal template.

## CTA Strategy Across Pages

CTAs soften as visitors go deeper into trust-building pages. Match commitment level to trust established.

| Page | CTA Approach | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| Homepage | High commitment | "Book your free assessment" |
| Service page | Medium commitment | "See if this is right for you" |
| About page | Low commitment | "Talk to our team" |
| Team bio | Personal, lowest friction | "Work with [Name]" |
| Case study | Conversational | "Is your situation similar?" |
| Resources | Lead capture | "Get the full guide" |
| Contact page | Direct, no softening | Single, clear calendar embed |

**Never use generic CTAs.** "Contact Us" on every page regardless of page context is a template tell. An About page visitor and a Services page visitor are at different temperatures.

## Sticky Global Conversion Element

Every inner page must have a persistent path to the primary conversion goal. The visitor must never have to go back to the homepage to find out how to contact the firm.

Implementation options (pick one):
1. CTA button in navigation bar (highest visibility — recommended)
2. Sticky sidebar element with advisor headshot and "Talk to us" — Aspiriant model
3. Exit-intent popup tied to scroll depth (last resort)

---

## Internal Linking Rule

Every inner page must contain at minimum:
1. One link to a related service or topic page
2. One link deeper into the trust stack (About if on Services; Case Studies if on About)
3. One CTA to the primary conversion destination

No page is complete without all three. No dead ends.

**Cross-linking patterns:**
- Service pages → Case Studies + Team bios
- Team bios → Services + Contact
- Blog posts → Relevant service pages + assessment CTA
- Case studies → Related service page + Contact

Every page reachable in 2 clicks from homepage.

## Agent Build Rules

Apply these when building any inner page:

**1. Belief-bridge test:** Before building, identify (a) what the visitor believes when they arrive, (b) what they need to believe to take the next step, and (c) what that next step is. The page bridges (a) to (b); the CTA delivers (b) to action.

**2. CTA calibration:** Match commitment level of CTA to trust established on that page. Higher-trust pages (Case Studies, Team bios) → higher-commitment CTAs. First-visit service pages → lower-commitment CTAs.

**3. About page opening rule:** Never start with founding year, AUM, or credential list. Start with the problem the firm was created to solve.

**4. Service page opening rule:** First sentence must name the client's pain. Not the service name.

**5. Team bio rule:** Every advisor bio must include a personal paragraph. No exceptions.

**6. Resources taxonomy rule:** Filter categories must mirror client problems, not content types.

**7. Contact page rule:** Calendar embed above the fold. Form fields: name, email, one qualifying question. Phone and address in footer only.

**8. Case study compliance rule:** Client archetypes only (no real names without consent). Format: Situation → Challenge → Solution → Measurable Outcome. CTA after every case study references the client's situation.

**9. Navigation build order:** Lock 5–7 primary nav items first. Assign every page to nav or footer. Client Login = utility element, not nav item. Footer must include full sitemap + all compliance links.

**10. No orphaned pages:** Every page has a CTA, internal links, and a forward path. A page without these is not complete.

## Common Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Fix |
|-------------|-----|
| Brochure trap — describe the firm instead of the client situation | Test: can a prospect see themselves in 10 seconds? If no, rewrite. |
| Credentials first — leading with designations before establishing the problem | Credentials = proof, not positioning. Move to middle or end. |
| Generic CTAs on every page | Match CTA commitment level to page temperature |
| Long intake forms on contact pages | Name, email, one question. Discovery happens on the call. |
| No case studies or proof on service pages | One real-world scenario minimum per service page |
| Team page with headshots and credentials only | Add personal paragraph to every bio — it's the conversion driver |
| Blog with no conversion mechanism | Every article → one contextual CTA back to services or assessment |
| Navigation overload — more than 7 primary items | Cut to 5–7 items; move rest to footer |
| "Our X" naming in nav — "Our Approach", "Our Process", "Our Story" | Rename to client-benefit language: "How We Work", "Your Roadmap" |
| Client Login in primary nav | Move to top-right utility position, separate from conversion nav |
