The real math behind a 7-year forgivable note.

Transition mathJuly 8, 2026

A large check can be attractive. The obligation attached to it deserves the same attention as the headline number.

▶

Episode transcript

A forgivable note is often presented as transition money. Economically, it is closer to a loan that is repaid through time. A portion is forgiven as you remain with the firm and satisfy the terms. Leave early, miss a hurdle, or trigger another repayment clause, and the unforgiven balance may come due.

The headline number is not the whole offer.

Start by separating the gross note from what you actually keep. The relevant questions are how long the note runs, how forgiveness occurs, whether forgiveness is straight-line or back-loaded, and which events can stop it.

A note that looks generous on day one can reduce your options for years. That does not make it a bad deal. It means the flexibility you give up should be part of the valuation.

Model the balance at more than one exit date.

Run the math as if you left after year two, year three, and year five. Include the remaining principal, any interest provision, repayment timing, and the tax already paid on forgiven portions. The exercise shows whether the offer still works if the firm, leadership, or business model changes.

The useful number is not only what you receive. It is what remains owed at the point your circumstances may change.

Read the clauses that can change the outcome.

  • The forgiveness schedule and whether it depends on production or asset hurdles.
  • The definition of voluntary and involuntary departure.
  • What happens after a change in control, acquisition, or role change.
  • Interest, repayment deadlines, and the firm’s enforcement rights.
  • The payout-grid economics across the entire term.

A bigger check is not automatically a better fit.

One model may maximize cash at transition. Another may prioritize ongoing economics, control, or enterprise value. Compare the note with the business model that supports your clients, team, and long-term practice. The right answer may still be to sign, but it should be a decision made with the liability visible.
