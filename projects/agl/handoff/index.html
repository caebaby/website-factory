02The pain · the four questions

The questions you can’t ask out loud.

Comp grids, mergers, recruiter noise — those are the triggers. Underneath them, every advisor weighing a move is stuck on the same four questions. All four are answerable. Just not by anyone with a stake in your answer.

“Can my book actually move?”

The fear says it’s a leap. Your contract says it’s a fact — non-solicit scope, comp language, how each relationship was papered. Some books genuinely can’t move, and you deserve to hear that straight. Most advisors never find out either way. We read yours before you risk anything.

“What’s the real math — not the recruiter’s version?”

You’ve run their headline number at midnight and felt the catch: the bonus is a leash, the clawbacks outlive the goodwill, the grid quietly takes the check back. Real math is boring — annualized, netted, ugly columns included — and it’s the only version worth deciding on.

“Who can I even ask without tipping my hand?”

Twenty years in, and you’re researching your own future from a throwaway account — because one wrong question in the wrong room makes you “disloyal.” Planning your own life shouldn’t feel like espionage. Everything here is private by design: nothing shared, no one notified. Ever.

“What am I actually building here?”

Watch a 30-year vet get managed out — clients reassigned by Friday — and the answer lands: someone else’s asset. That’s the line-item feeling, and it doesn’t retire with you. The alternative is a book you own on paper. One you could someday sell — not just leave.

04The solution · the third door

You’ve been shown two doors: the quota, or the wilderness.

Every forum thread paints the same two futures: stay captive, or go it completely alone — your own compliance, your own tech, your own everything. Nobody shows the third door, because the loudest voices only walked through the first two. There’s a model in between — and in Fidelity’s research, independence is where movers keep landing, and where they report being happier and more in control.

The two doors you know

The quota — or the wilderness

  • A 45–55% payout — on a grid the firm can change under you
  • The firm owns the client relationships you built
  • 7-to-9-year notes with clawbacks, even if they terminate you
  • Production hurdles just to keep your own assistant
  • Weeks of home-office review for one piece of marketing
  • At the end: a retirement payout — not a business you sell
The third door

How the model actually works

  • Your book is yours by contract — client list, files, brand. Not a promise. Paper.
  • An asset you can actually sell — recurring fee-based revenue that compounds into enterprise value at publicly reported multiples
  • Payouts publicly discussed in the ~90% range for independent models — illustrative, not an offer
  • An enterprise back office runs the machinery — compliance, technology, planning ops, staff — the exact things solo advisors drown in
  • Your name on the door — your brand, your practice, run your way
  • Advisors beside you who made this exact move — volunteers, not hostages

Independence was never the problem. Alone was. Whether the third door fits your book is a 30-minute diagnostic — not a pitch.
